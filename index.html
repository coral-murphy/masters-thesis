By Coral Murphy Marcos

On a warm January evening, Wanda Arzuaga López made her way through a neighborhood in southeastern Juncos, Puerto Rico, as she campaigned for the position of the town’s next mayor.

Sporting a light blue polo, silver hoop earrings, and a white canvas tote adorned with the message of her campaign principles, which also happens to be her party’s name: Proyecto Dignindad, or The Dignity Project, in English.

Joined by her campaign director and four dedicated volunteers, Arzuaga López goes door-to-door, warmly exclaiming, “Hi there! May God bless your family!” in a sector where the majority of residents, most of them above their 50s, have borne the brunt of living near a waste disposal facility nestled within Puerto Rico’s lush greenery.

For Arzuaga López, the hardships endured by the people in her hometown extend far beyond the acrid air. She aims to connect with potential voters disenchanted by Puerto Rico's current political landscape.

Economic challenges disproportionately impact low-income communities in Puerto Rico, where over 40 percent of the island's 3.2 million inhabitants live below the poverty line, exacerbated by the devastating aftermath of Hurricane María in 2017.

For the mayoral hopeful in Juncos, this is why more than half of the town's eligible voters abstained from participating in the mayoral election in 2020. With economic, political, and environmental woes coming at Puerto Ricans from all sides, the pendulum is swinging to the right.

Traditionally a two-party system, Puerto Rico's political discourse was disrupted when a non-elected fiscal control board, imposed by former President Barack Obama, eroded the island's autonomy. About a year later, Hurricane Maria devastated the island, causing enduring issues like a fragile power grid and frequent outages, aggravated by the pandemic and austerity measures.

Corruption, Puerto Rico’s much too often plague du jour, was pronounced most acutely in 2019, when the island garnered international attention after thousands of protesters demonstrated in the streets of San Juan calling for the resignation of former governor Ricardo Rosselló. Amidst this turmoil, Proyecto Dignidad, a conservative party, has gained traction.

The church, once confined to private life, is now a political force dictating policies and reshaping community dynamics. Arzuaga López, a chaplain at a local church, remembers when she helped her mother sue the water authority for its inefficient service for residents living in mountainous, hard-to-reach areas in the early 2000s.

Proyecto Dignidad, the political party at the forefront of this shift, is actively canvassing often-neglected municipalities. Women, teenagers, and pastors are convening at local plazas to hear how Proyecto Dignidad strives to eradicate Puerto Rico’s financial distress and high criminality rates, issues of pressing concern all over the island, while promising to reverse policy that allows abortions in Puerto Rico and protects the rights of the LGBTQ+ communities.

The incumbent mayor of Juncos, a white-mustached man bearing the nickname “Papo,” was picked by 23 percent of eligible voters. He belongs to the Popular Democratic Party, which has historically advocated for Puerto Rico’s commonwealth status, or what locals call “the status quo.” It’s also what others call a colonial limbo.

In the 2020 elections, Proyecto Dignidad unexpectedly secured two conservative female lawmakers, focusing on anti-abortion, anti-gender education, and anti-street harassment policies. Four years later, the party is substantially much stronger and plans to secure even more spots in government.

In 2020, the party debuted with 29 candidates running for positions including governor, mayor, local Senate and House seats. Four years later, that number has spiked to 401.
